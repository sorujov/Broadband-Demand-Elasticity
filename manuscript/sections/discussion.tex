\subsection{Policy Implications for Digital Inclusion}

Our findings have direct implications for broadband policy in Europe and neighboring regions. The strong pre-COVID price elasticity in EaP countries suggests that affordability-focused policies---price regulation, operator subsidies, or consumer vouchers---can effectively increase broadband adoption. A 10\% price reduction would increase subscriptions by approximately $\EaPTenPctChg$\%, translating to nearly \EaPAddSubsTotal{} new users across the EaP region.

However, the shift toward inelastic demand during COVID-19 fundamentally alters the policy toolkit. When price sensitivity approaches zero, further price reductions yield minimal adoption gains. Instead, policies must address other barriers including infrastructure gaps (particularly in rural areas), digital literacy deficits, and content relevance \citep{guermazi2021broadband}. The transition from price-sensitive to price-insensitive demand marks broadband's evolution from discretionary service to essential utility.

For EU countries, the consistently low elasticity even pre-COVID suggests price is not the primary adoption barrier. With subscription rates exceeding \DescSubsEU{} per 100 inhabitants (Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}), most households with willingness to pay already have broadband. Remaining gaps likely reflect supply constraints (rural coverage) or demographic factors (elderly populations). EU policy should focus on universal service obligations ensuring infrastructure availability rather than price interventions.

The EaP-EU differential highlights the importance of context-dependent policy design. Uniform European approaches may be suboptimal given heterogeneous demand elasticities. EaP countries benefit more from affordability programs, while EU countries require infrastructure and digital skills initiatives.

\subsection{Time-Varying Elasticity and Demand Evolution}

A central finding is that broadband demand elasticity is not constant but evolves systematically over time. For EU countries (N$=$27), the year-by-year analysis reveals a statistically precise declining trend starting well before COVID-19, with elasticity falling from $\EUYbyYRefElast$ in 2015 to near zero by the late 2010s. For EaP countries, year-by-year estimates cannot be interpreted at the annual frequency: with only five to six EaP countries, each triple interaction term is identified from too few cross-sectional units and is confounded with EaP-bloc-specific shocks (geopolitical events, investment cycles) that cannot be separated from the price response. Instead, the pooled placebo test (Table~\ref{tab:placebo}) confirms that EaP elasticity was also declining before COVID-19 ($+\PlaceboEaP$, p$=$\PlaceboEaPPval), as was EU elasticity ($+\PlaceboEU$, p$=$\PlaceboEUPval). This gradual transformation in both regions suggests demand elasticity is a function of market maturity, technological change, and societal integration.

Three mechanisms likely drive this evolution. First, \textbf{network effects} become stronger as broadband penetration increases. The value of connectivity rises with the number of connected users, making broadband more essential regardless of price \citep{katz1985network}. Second, \textbf{habit formation} makes broadband increasingly indispensable as consumers integrate it into daily routines \citep{becker1988theory}. Third, \textbf{complementary innovations}---smartphones, streaming services, cloud computing---increase broadband's value proposition independent of price.

This time-varying elasticity has important methodological implications. Static elasticity estimates from early-period data may not apply to current markets. Panel methods allowing for time variation, as employed here, are essential for capturing evolving demand patterns.

From a forecasting perspective, our results suggest broadband adoption will continue even without further price declines. The near-zero COVID-era elasticity implies that infrastructure availability and service quality---not affordability---now determine adoption. Countries planning broadband expansion should prioritize network deployment over price regulation as markets mature.

\subsection{Price Measurement and Affordability Metrics}

The robustness across price measures (Table~\ref{tab:price_robustness}) provides valuable methodological insights. All three price definitions---income-relative (GNI\%), PPP-adjusted, and nominal USD---yield negative elasticity estimates, confirming that broadband demand is price-sensitive regardless of measurement choice. However, the magnitudes differ substantially: income-relative prices yield the largest EaP elasticities ($\EaPGNIFCB$), followed by nominal USD ($\EaPUSDBCB$), and PPP-adjusted ($\EaPPPPFCB$).

However, \textit{income-relative prices} emerge as the most appropriate affordability metric. The ITU basket is designed to track the minimum viable entry-level broadband offering in each period---1\,GB in the early years, 5\,GB once that became the prevailing threshold---so the GNI\% measure is literally the fraction of monthly income a household must spend to cross the adoption threshold. This directly matches the object of interest in a discrete adoption model: not how expensive broadband is in absolute terms, but whether it is affordable enough to subscribe at all \citep{deaton1980almost}. A \$30/month connection is inexpensive for a household earning \$3{,}000/month but prohibitive for one earning \$200/month; GNI\% captures this heterogeneity while nominal USD does not.

This framing also clarifies the PPP failure. PPP deflators are constructed from consumer expenditure baskets dominated by food, 
housing, and tradable goods---categories where quality-adjusted prices are stable. 
Technology services, by contrast, exhibit rapid quality-adjusted price declines \citep{schreyer2002oecd}. 
Applying PPP to broadband prices therefore introduces a systematic deflation error, 
understating the purchasing-power cost of connectivity in lower-income countries and 
reducing the precision of cross-country affordability comparisons. 
Income-relative pricing sidesteps this entirely by using income---rather than a mismeasured price index---as the denominator.

Empirically, income-relative prices show the strongest relationship with subscriptions (highest $R^2$ values across specifications), 
as demonstrated in Figure~\ref{fig:price_measurement}. This validates the ITU's practice of expressing 
affordability targets as percentages of GNI per capita \citep{itu2024data}. Policymakers tracking broadband affordability 
should prioritize income-relative metrics over both nominal and PPP-adjusted prices.

An important caveat is that our price data represent standardized fixed-broadband 
baskets whose definition 
changed over the sample: the ITU's 1\,GB basket (I154\_FBB, 2010--2017) was succeeded by 
its 5\,GB basket (I154\_FBB5, 2018--2024) as 5\,GB became the prevailing entry-level threshold. 
Because this transition occurred simultaneously for all countries in 2018, year fixed effects absorb 
the common level shift, so the basket redefinition does not confound within-country elasticity estimates. 
Actual consumer prices vary by speed tier, data caps, and bundling arrangements beyond what standardized baskets capture. 
To the extent measurement error exists, classical error would attenuate estimates toward zero, 
making our significant negative elasticities conservative. 
Future research could refine price measurement using micro-level tariff data, 
though cross-country comparability would be challenging.

\subsection{Regional Heterogeneity and Development Gradients}

The large EaP-EU elasticity differential reflects broader development gradients. EaP countries have lower GDP per capita (\$4,500--\$13,000 PPP) compared to EU countries (\$25,000--\$60,000 PPP), translating directly to affordability constraints and higher price sensitivity.

Beyond income, institutional and infrastructure differences matter. EaP countries exhibit lower regulatory quality (Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}), potentially reducing competitive pressure and raising prices. Weaker governance may also hamper policy implementation of broadband programs. Additionally, lower internet server density and R\&D intensity suggest less developed digital ecosystems, reducing complementary services that drive demand.

These structural differences imply that development strategies successful in high-income EU markets may not transfer directly to middle-income EaP contexts. Targeted approaches addressing affordability constraints and institutional gaps are essential.

Interestingly, the convergence toward near-zero elasticity during COVID occurred in both regions, suggesting the pandemic's impacts transcended development levels. This may reflect the universal need for connectivity during social restrictions, overriding typical income-based differences in demand.

\subsection{COVID-19 Shock vs. Secular Trends}

A crucial interpretive question is whether observed changes reflect a COVID-19 shock or
continuation of pre-existing trends. The placebo test (Table~\ref{tab:placebo}) provides
suggestive evidence for the latter: both EU and EaP
placebo interactions are positive and marginally significant, indicating that elasticity
was already declining during 2015--2019.

The period-split results (Table~\ref{tab:period_split}) sharpen this interpretation by
decomposing the post-2019 period into two distinct phases. During the \emph{acute} COVID
phase (2020--2021), lockdowns and remote-work mandates plausibly converted broadband from
a convenience to a necessity, suppressing price sensitivity. During the \emph{post-acute}
phase (2022--2024), pandemic restrictions eased and work/education patterns largely normalized
in most sample countries. Yet near-zero elasticity persisted. This persistence is
inconsistent with a pure pandemic-shock interpretation---if the shock were the mechanism,
one would expect some reversion toward pre-COVID elasticity as restrictions lifted.

Instead, zero post-acute elasticity supports the \emph{secular transformation} hypothesis:
broadband has permanently crossed the threshold from discretionary to essential service.
High-speed connectivity is now deeply embedded
in work, education, healthcare, and social life. Even at elevated prices, households treat
disconnection as prohibitively costly---analogous to electricity or water utilities. COVID-19
did not cause this transition; it accelerated the final stage of a process underway since
roughly 2015.

For policy, this distinction has permanent implications. If zero elasticity is durable,
price subsidies lose their effectiveness. The binding
constraints are now infrastructure availability (especially in rural areas),
digital literacy, and service quality. EU policy should focus
on universal coverage; EaP policy should pair infrastructure investment with
institutional reforms to foster competition.

\subsection{Limitations and Alternative Explanations}

Several limitations qualify our findings. First, \textbf{aggregation}: country-level data obscure within-country heterogeneity. Urban-rural gaps, income distribution, and demographic differences are masked. Micro-level household data would enable richer analysis of heterogeneous demand, though cross-country micro data covering 33 countries over 15 years are unavailable.

Second, \textbf{fixed vs.\ mobile broadband}: our analysis focuses on fixed broadband, which
offers the longest consistent time series for cross-country comparison. Mobile broadband has
expanded rapidly---particularly in middle-income EaP countries---and may substitute for fixed
connections at lower price points \citep{gruber2014mobile}. To assess whether fixed-broadband
demand is confounded by mobile substitution, we re-estimated the baseline specification
adding log mobile subscriptions per 100 inhabitants as a control. Elasticity estimates are
stable (EU: $-0.10$; EaP: $-0.60$), indicating mobile expansion does not materially bias
the fixed-broadband elasticity. For bandwidth-intensive applications central to the COVID
adoption story (video streaming, remote work, distance learning), fixed broadband remains
the relevant margin.

Third, \textbf{quality variation}: ITU price data reflect standardized entry-level baskets (1\,GB through 2017, 5\,GB from 2018), but actual services vary widely in speed, data caps, and reliability. Consumers may respond differently to price changes depending on quality. Lack of quality-adjusted prices is a common limitation in telecommunications research \citep{greenstein2016measuring}.

Fourth, \textbf{endogeneity}: despite extensive controls and fixed effects, simultaneity
between prices and quantities could bias estimates. Operators may set prices based on
expected demand, inducing correlation. While regulatory frameworks limit short-run price
adjustment, reducing simultaneity concerns \citep{laffont2000competition}, we address this
directly using 2SLS instrumenting fixed broadband prices with their one-year lag
(Table~\ref{tab:iv_robustness}; within-demeaned first-stage $F = \IVFirstF$ pre-COVID,
$F = \IVFullF$ full-sample). The pre-COVID lagged-price 2SLS yields EU$=\IVPreEUB$
(SE$=\IVPreEUSE$, p$=$0.075) and EaP$=\IVPreEaPB$ (SE$=\IVPreEaPSE$, p$<$0.001),
virtually identical to OLS, providing direct evidence that simultaneity bias is negligible.
The full-sample 2SLS (Panel~B) yields EU$=\IVFullEUB$ (SE$=\IVFullEUSE$, p$<$0.001)
and EaP$=\IVFullEaPB$ (SE$=\IVFullEaPSE$, p$<$0.001), consistent with the COVID-era
attenuation of price sensitivity found in OLS. An alternative bandwidth instrument yields
a larger EaP magnitude and the over-identified specification rejects the Hansen $J$ test,
confirming lagged price as the preferred instrument. Overall, the IV results validate
the OLS estimates: the endogeneity concern is resolved.

Fifth, \textbf{external validity}: findings derive from European and Eastern Partnership countries. Generalization to other regions---particularly low-income countries in Africa, Asia, or Latin America---is uncertain. Demand elasticity likely varies with income, institutions, and technology. Cross-regional studies are needed to assess global applicability.

Alternative explanations for near-zero COVID-era elasticity merit consideration. Beyond necessity-good interpretation, three mechanisms could generate observed patterns:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supply constraints:} If operators could not deliver additional capacity during COVID, quantity might be supply-determined rather than demand-determined. However, most countries increased network capacity rapidly \citep{oecd2021broadband}, suggesting supply was not binding.
    
    \item \textbf{Measurement timing:} Annual data may miss within-year dynamics. If price and quantity adjusted at different times within years, relationships could appear weak. Higher-frequency (monthly) data would clarify dynamics, though cross-country monthly data are unavailable.
    
    \item \textbf{Government interventions:} Some countries implemented broadband subsidies or price controls during COVID \citep{oecd2021broadband}. If subsidies offset price increases, observed relationships could be distorted. Detailed policy data would help control for interventions.
\end{itemize}

Despite these limitations, the robustness across specifications, price measures (Table~\ref{tab:price_robustness}), and time periods provides confidence in core findings.

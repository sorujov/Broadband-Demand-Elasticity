# Assessment of Publication Prospects in Information Economics and Policy

## 1. Context and Question

The manuscript "The Transformation of Broadband Demand: From Discretionary Service to Essential Infrastructure (2010–2024)" analyzes time-varying broadband price elasticity for EU and Eastern Partnership (EaP) countries using a 15‑year panel and two-way fixed effects with Driscoll–Kraay standard errors. The author seeks an assessment of the manuscript’s suitability and likely publication prospects in *Information Economics and Policy* (IEP).[^1]

## 2. Fit with IEP Aims and Scope

IEP positions itself as a Q1 journal at the intersection of information/communication technologies, industrial organization, and public policy, publishing empirical and theoretical work on telecommunications markets, broadband, digital infrastructure, and regulation. The journal has a track record of papers on broadband adoption, willingness-to-pay for speed, universal service, and market definition in broadband markets.[^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]

Within this portfolio, the manuscript’s focus on broadband demand, price elasticity, and policy implications for universal service and affordability is clearly aligned with IEP’s core topics. The explicit engagement with IEP-published work (e.g., Liu, Prince and Wallsten; Lindlacher; Grzybowski et al.) strengthens topical fit and signals awareness of the journal’s audience.[^3][^6][^1][^2]

## 3. Contribution Relative to Existing IEP Literature

IEP and nearby outlets already host several strands of broadband-related work: household WTP and speed–latency trade-offs, demand for high-speed vs basic internet, universal service and entry subsidies, and demand for fixed–mobile bundles. These papers typically estimate demand or WTP at a point in time or for a relatively short horizon and focus on specific countries or speed tiers.[^6][^2][^3]

The present manuscript differentiates itself on at least three dimensions:

- **Time-varying elasticity over a long panel (2010–2024):** Year-by-year and period-wise estimates showing a secular decline of price elasticity and convergence to near-zero post‑2020 are new relative to prior IEP work that treats elasticity as time-invariant.[^1][^2]
- **Regional comparative angle (EU vs EaP):** The explicit comparison between high-income EU and middle-income EaP markets, with large elasticity differences pre‑COVID and convergence during COVID, adds a development and integration dimension largely absent in existing EU‑focused IEP papers.[^6][^1]
- **Price measurement critique (GNI% vs PPP vs nominal):** The systematic comparison of income-relative, PPP-adjusted, and nominal prices, with strong evidence that income-relative measures dominate for policy inference in EaP countries, is a methodological contribution relevant for many ICT demand studies.[^7][^1]

Given IEP’s history of hosting methodologically careful demand work with policy angles, these contributions appear novel enough to pass an originality screen, provided the exposition remains tightly connected to existing IEP articles and highlights incremental value clearly in the introduction and conclusion.[^2][^3][^6]

## 4. Econometric Rigor and Identification

The empirical strategy—two-way fixed effects with country and year FE, Driscoll–Kraay standard errors, rich controls, and extensive robustness checks—meets and in some respects exceeds the econometric standards of typical empirical papers in IEP. Key strengths include:[^1][^2]

- Use of a balanced 33‑country panel over 15 years with detailed construction and discussion of ITU and World Bank indicators, including variable transformations, missing data treatment, and quality checks.[^1]
- Careful treatment of error structure with Driscoll–Kraay SEs to handle heteroskedasticity, serial correlation, and cross-sectional dependence, which is particularly relevant in the presence of common shocks like COVID‑19.[^1]
- Multiple robustness dimensions: alternative control sets, price measures, subsamples by period, placebo tests for pre-trends, period-split (acute/post-acute COVID), 2SLS checks with mobile-broadband prices as instruments, and leave-one-country-out jackknife for EaP.[^1]

The manuscript also engages with recent critiques of two-way fixed-effects under treatment heterogeneity (Goodman-Bacon, de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfœuille) and explains why the problematic staggered-DiD setting does not apply here due to continuous regressors and common timing of COVID-period interactions. This level of econometric self-awareness is likely to be valued by IEP referees.[^1]

Potential points where referees may still push include:

- The strength and interpretability of the mobile-price instrument, especially for EaP-specific interactions, given the reported weak first stage in some specifications.[^1]
- The degree to which country-year level data can distinguish demand shocks from concurrent supply-side and policy shocks, particularly during COVID and in conflict-affected EaP countries.

These are not fatal but may trigger a round of revision and additional sensitivity checks.

## 5. Policy Relevance and Narrative

IEP favors work that not only identifies demand parameters but draws explicit lessons for competition policy, universal service, infrastructure investment, and affordability programs. The manuscript speaks directly to this by:[^8][^3][^2]

- Interpreting the pre‑COVID EaP elasticity (about −0.60) in terms of the number of additional connections generated by a 10% price cut and the revenue trade-off for operators.[^1]
- Arguing that the shift to near-zero elasticity implies diminishing returns to price subsidies and a need to reorient policy toward infrastructure deployment, rural coverage, and digital literacy as broadband becomes an essential utility.[^9][^1]
- Framing the COVID period as an accelerator of an underlying secular trend rather than a one-off shock, which matters for the long-run design of digital inclusion policies.

The policy discussion is relatively extensive by economics-journal standards and is directly targeted at regulators, operators, and international institutions, which matches IEP’s readership. Clarity might improve if some micro-detail from the robustness matrix is compressed and those pages reallocated to more concrete policy scenarios or back-of-the-envelope welfare calculations.[^5][^1]

## 6. Presentation, Length, and Positioning

IEP articles are typically concise empirical pieces (often around 8–10k words) with focused contributions and relatively compact appendices, though the journal does not publicly list a strict word limit. The current manuscript is long, with a very detailed methodology and robustness appendices effectively integrated into the main text.[^5][^1]

From an editor’s perspective, the main risks on presentation are:

- **Perceived length and density:** The level of detail in methodological sections (e.g., long discussions of Driscoll–Kraay mechanics, TWFE heterogeneity issues, and multiple robustness matrices) may be seen as exceeding what is necessary for a field-journal audience.
- **Repetition in sections 5–6:** Some messages (declining elasticity, COVID vs secular trend, GNI% vs PPP) are repeated with similar figures and narrative.

These can be mitigated by:

- Moving some of the more technical econometric discussion and full robustness grids to an online appendix, keeping only the essential intuition and key tables/figures in the main text.
- Tightening the literature review by focusing on the subset of IEP and top-field papers that are closest competitors and using others more sparingly.
- Sharpening the “hook” in the introduction and conclusion to foreground 2–3 main contributions, with everything else clearly labeled as supporting evidence.

If these adjustments are made, the paper’s readability and editor/referee goodwill are likely to improve.

## 7. Journal Competitiveness and Baseline Acceptance Chances

IEP is a Q1 economics journal with an impact factor in the 3–5 range and a high CiteScore and SJR, implying a competitive submissions environment. Like comparable field journals in industrial organization and ICT policy, informal evidence suggests a low overall acceptance rate (likely well below one-third), though precise public figures are not available.[^4][^7]

Recent IEP broadband papers are methodologically solid, tightly written, and clearly positioned, reflecting relatively high standards for acceptance. The current manuscript’s topic and methods are in line with this standard, but publication remains uncertain due to competition from other strong submissions and editorial preferences at the time of submission.[^3][^2][^6]

Under these conditions, and conditional on careful tightening and positioning, the manuscript appears to be in the range where a serious revise-and-resubmit is plausible at IEP rather than being clearly out-of-scope or below the bar. However, no ex-ante assessment can meaningfully raise the expected probability above “non-trivial but materially less than 50%,” given the field journal’s competitiveness and the inherent variance in referee reports.

## 8. Main Factors Increasing or Decreasing Probability

### 8.1 Positive factors

- **Excellent topical match:** Broadband demand, universal service, and digital-inclusion policy are central to IEP’s mission and recent publications.[^8][^2][^3]
- **Clear incremental contribution:** Long panel, time-varying elasticity, EU–EaP comparison, and price-measurement critique all extend what existing IEP papers offer.[^2][^6][^1]
- **High econometric standard:** Thoughtful identification discussion, Driscoll–Kraay inference, rich robustness, and engagement with recent TWFE critiques position the paper as technically serious.[^1]
- **Strong policy narrative:** The elasticity results are explicitly translated into implications for subsidies vs infrastructure investments, in a style consistent with IEP.[^9][^1]

### 8.2 Potential concerns

- **Length and density:** The manuscript may be perceived as too long and technically dense for IEP’s usual format unless streamlined.
- **Macro vs micro identification:** Country-level panels necessarily blend demand, supply, and policy shocks; some referees may prefer household microdata or quasi-experimental designs.
- **COVID interpretation:** Although the paper is careful, COVID remains a crowded research space; editors may worry about “COVID fatigue” unless the secular-transformation angle is strongly emphasized.
- **Regional focus:** While EU‑plus‑EaP is relevant, some referees may see external validity limits; positioning the paper as an EU‑adjacent case study with broader methodological lessons can mitigate this.

## 9. Concrete Recommendations Before Submission

1. **Sharpen the contribution statement** in the introduction to three bullets explicitly framed relative to prior IEP work (time-varying elasticity, EU–EaP heterogeneity, price-measurement critique). Cite the key IEP precedents directly there.[^3][^6][^2]
2. **Condense econometric exposition** by moving most of the Driscoll–Kraay formulae, TWFE discussion, and the full 24‑specification robustness matrix into an online appendix, retaining only the intuition and headline robustness results in the main text.[^1]
3. **Prioritize a small set of figures/tables** (e.g., baseline elasticity table, COVID vs pre-COVID plot, year-by-year EU elasticity chart, and a compact price-measurement table) and relegate secondary plots to the appendix.
4. **Clarify identification limits** around COVID and conflict shocks in a short subsection, emphasizing that results are descriptive-structural (panel demand estimates) rather than quasi-experimental causal effects of specific policies.
5. **Tighten the policy section** by adding one or two calibrated scenarios (e.g., welfare impact of a given price subsidy vs an infrastructure grant, or the implied deadweight loss under zero elasticity) to concretize recommendations.[^9][^1]
6. **Check IEP formatting and data policies**, including expectations for data and code availability (e.g., linking the GitHub repository prominently) and ensure the replication package meets current norms.[^8][^5]

## 10. Overall Assessment

Taking all factors together—scope fit, contribution, econometric rigor, policy relevance, and presentation—the manuscript appears well suited for IEP in terms of topic and methodological approach and is clearly above the threshold where submission is warranted.[^2][^1]

However, IEP’s competitiveness implies that even well-aligned and competently executed papers often receive “reject and resubmit elsewhere” decisions, especially if they are perceived as too long or somewhat incremental relative to existing work. Streamlining the exposition, sharpening the value added relative to recent IEP broadband papers, and clarifying the secular-transformation angle should materially improve the probability of at least receiving a revise-and-resubmit rather than a desk rejection.[^7][^4]

On balance, conditional on implementing the suggested tightening and positioning, the ex-ante probability of eventual acceptance at IEP is best described as **meaningfully positive but still below one-half**, which is consistent with the journal’s field and ranking: high enough to justify targeting IEP first, but low enough that a fallback strategy (e.g., *Telecommunications Policy* or similar) should be planned in parallel.
